From PM to Oiler: What I Learned Crossing Industries and Oceans

In 2026, abandoning a tech career for a trade is very much the fashionable thing to do. The logic goes that robotics are further from meaningful industrial automation than, say, jobs done from a laptop — so trades are the safer bet. I'd argue it's a short-sighted one. The average trade takes a few years to reach journeyman status, and if the last few years have taught us anything, a lot can happen in a few years.

For me though, this had nothing to do with the AI apocalypse and everything to do with freedom and a mild existential crisis.

In early 2024, I was at my third FAANG, pulling a very comfortable six-figure TC, and had been fully remote for eight years. By every metric I'd set for myself at 22, I'd made it. So naturally, something had to break.

I was in a cute Airbnb in Poros, Greece. 11PM local. 17 degrees. Ninety-four meters away, a very enthusiastic solstice party was happening without me — because I was on my fifth call of the day. Three people. A project with "unicorn potential" that would almost certainly never ship. Standard stuff. Then a frequent collaborator joined and announced himself: "Hello, this is John Doe."

He did this on every call. Including one-on-ones. Meet tells you. The app announces it. His face is on the screen. He announces it anyway.

Something snapped.

Two minutes in, a deep wave of absolutely not washed over me. By minute five I was mentally drafting my resignation. By the end of the call I was looking at flights to extend my stay in Greece.

I planned to bring it up at my regular Monday one-on-one. Then, at 3:45PM PST that Friday, I got a calendar invite from my manager and someone from People Ops for 4:30PM — that day.

On the call: I was being offered a promotion. In-person. Managerial. My current role was being eliminated as part of a restructure. I could move into the new role or explore other internal options. Then People Ops outlined an exit package: six months salary, a year of benefits, and full vesting of all outstanding restricted units. All in, it was nearly four years of total comp to simply walk away.

The room expected me to take the promotion. My energy was positive — for very different reasons.

When they asked if I had any questions, I had one: "Are you happy?"

They seemed mildly thrown off. Gave pleasant non-answers.

"I don't think I am anymore," I said. "I'll take the exit package."

Thirty days later I was back in the US, doing exit interviews, returning six devices, and starting over completely.
